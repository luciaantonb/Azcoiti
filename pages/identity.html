Info

Before clothing became a product, it was a language. The way people dress is not simply a response to trends or temperature. It is one of the most immediate ways a person expresses who they are, how they feel, and how they want to move through the world.

A study of 792 women found that clothing choices reflect not just how people want to be perceived, but how they feel about themselves. People with greater openness to experience were 1.8 times more likely to develop an urban, sophisticated personal style. Those actively seeking individuality through clothing were 3.96 times more likely to develop a recognisable style of their own. And so many more variations.

This is clothing working as it was always meant to. Not as a trend object or a disposable purchase, but as something personal, considered, and specific to the person wearing it.

This pattern encodes that specificity: high variation because identity is individual, never uniform. The data here does not describe a problem. It describes something worth protecting.

Parameters
Rhythm 4 An approximation of 3.96: the multiplier showing how much more likely people who seek individuality are to develop their own style.
Direction 0.26 The average of 28.8% (comfort) and 23.5% (garment-person match), normalised.
Variation 0.79 High variation. Identity is individual, never uniform. No two people express themselves the same way, and the pattern reflects that.
Folds 2 Bilateral symmetry: the interior and exterior self. How we see ourselves and how we present ourselves, mirrored.
Rotation 1 Slight rotation. Identity evolves but does not reverse. A slow turn, not a full inversion.
